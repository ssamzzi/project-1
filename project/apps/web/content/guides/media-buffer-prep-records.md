# Media and Buffer Preparation Records

Media and buffer preparation records make troubleshooting possible. Without a record, a failed experiment can only be guessed at.

## What to record

Record reagent lot, final concentration, pH target, volume, date, preparer, sterilization method, storage condition, and any deviation from the usual recipe.

## Supplements

Supplements often have different storage conditions from the base medium. Record when they were thawed, added, filtered, or aliquoted.

## pH and osmolarity

For sensitive cultures or assays, pH and osmolarity can change interpretation. A recipe may be correct on paper but still unsuitable if preparation conditions drifted.

## Working stocks

For working stocks, record the source stock, dilution factor, final concentration, solvent, storage temperature, and expiration or review date.

## Practical checklist

- Keep recipes versioned.
- Record lot numbers for critical reagents.
- Note pH adjustment steps.
- Label storage condition clearly.
- Review old stocks before using them in important experiments.

## Why this matters

A complete preparation record helps separate biological variation from preparation variation. It also supports reproducibility when another person repeats the same setup.
